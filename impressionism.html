Impressionism

What is Impressionism?

Impressionism was an art movement that began in France in the 1860s. It was, in many ways, a rebellion against everything that official art institutions considered correct.

Before Impressionism, the standard for great painting meant smooth, invisible brushstrokes, careful detail, and subjects drawn from history or mythology. Impressionist painters rejected all of that. They went outside, set up their easels in real places, parks, riversides, busy streets, and tried to capture not the scene itself, but the way light fell on it at a specific moment in time.

The name "Impressionism" started as an insult. A critic mocked Claude Monet's painting Impression, Sunrise in 1874, saying it looked like an unfinished sketch, just an "impression." The artists took the label anyway and made it their own. Today it is one of the most beloved styles in the history of art.

What Makes It Different?

Three ideas that set Impressionism apart from everything that came before it.

01 Light as the Subject Impressionists were not painting objects, they were painting light. The same pond, the same haystack, the same cathedral could look completely different depending on the time of day, the weather, and the season. Monet famously painted the same haystacks dozens of times to show exactly this. The real subject was always light itself, and how it constantly transforms the world around us.
02 Loose, Visible Brushstrokes Earlier painters blended their colors so smoothly that no individual brushstroke was visible. Impressionists did the opposite, they worked quickly and left their marks on the canvas deliberately. Up close, a Monet painting can look like a chaos of dabs and smears. Step back, and it resolves into shimmering water or a sunlit garden. This technique was considered sloppy and unfinished at the time. It is now considered a breakthrough.
03 Ordinary Life as Subject Matter Impressionists didn't paint gods, saints, or historical battles. They painted what they saw around them: people having lunch at a restaurant, ballet dancers stretching before rehearsal, children playing in a park, a woman reading by a window. This was a radical shift. It said that ordinary life, the life most people were actually living, was worth preserving in paint.
The Artists

Three Painters to Know

Claude Monet

1840 - 1926

The most recognizable name in Impressionism. Monet spent his life studying how natural light transforms a scene. His Water Lilies series, painted in the garden he designed at Giverny, shows the same pond across different hours and seasons. He continued painting even as his eyesight deteriorated significantly in his later years.

Edgar Degas

1834 - 1917

Degas was drawn to movement and the human body in motion. He is best known for his paintings and sculptures of ballet dancers, not performing on stage, but practicing, resting, and adjusting their shoes in the wings. He had a way of catching people in unguarded, in-between moments that made his work feel almost like photography before photography could do it.

Pierre-Auguste Renoir

1841 - 1919

If Monet painted light and Degas painted movement, Renoir painted warmth. His paintings are full of social gatherings, laughter, outdoor meals, and people clearly enjoying themselves. He believed art should give pleasure. His Luncheon of the Boating Party captures friends in dappled afternoon sunlight in a way that still feels alive and immediate.

Featured Artwork

A Closer Look

Impressionism: Featured Work

Weeping Willow

Claude Monet - 1919
Oil on Canvas
Photographed at the Modern Art Museum of Fort Worth

Monet painted this willow tree in 1919, during one of the darkest periods France had known. World War I had ended the year before, leaving the country devastated. Monet, who had a son who served in the war, was grieving along with the rest of the nation.

The weeping willow has long been used as a symbol of mourning. Monet chose it deliberately. Look at how the long branches sweep downward, they almost look like a figure bent in sadness. The brushstrokes are turbulent and expressive, very different from his earlier, sunlit water lily paintings.

There are no people in this painting, no narrative, no action, just a tree. And yet it communicates grief more directly than most explicitly sad paintings. That is what Impressionism at its best could do: translate a feeling into color and texture without ever stating it outright.

Takeaway

Why Impressionism Still Matters

Art as Experience, Not Record

Impressionism made a permanent argument about what art is for. Before it, painting largely tried to record the world, to show what something looked like. After Impressionism, painting could show what something felt like. How a morning in a garden felt. How grief felt. How a fleeting second of afternoon light felt before it was gone.

That shift, from recording to experiencing, changed everything that came after it. Abstract art, modern photography, and the entire idea of capturing mood rather than fact all trace back to what the Impressionists were doing in the parks and gardens of 1870s France.
