Impressionism

Light. Color. Feeling. Impressionism revolutionized art by painting the world not as it is, but as it feels in a fleeting moment of sunlight.

The Basics

What is Impressionism?

Impressionism was an art movement that began in France in the 1860s and 1870s. It was a radical break from everything that came before it.

Before Impressionism, painting was all about precision, perfectly blended colors, photographic detail, and grand historical or mythological scenes. Impressionist artists threw those rules out. Instead, they went outside, set up their easels in gardens and parks and riversides, and tried to capture exactly what their eyes saw in that exact moment, including how light shimmered, flickered, and changed.

The name "Impressionism" was actually meant as an insult. A critic mocked Claude Monet's painting Impression, Sunrise by calling it just an "impression", not a finished work of art. The artists embraced the name proudly. Today it's one of the most beloved art styles in the world.

Key Ideas

What Makes Impressionism Unique?

Three core ideas define Impressionist paintings.

  • *
    Capturing Light and the Moment Impressionists were obsessed with light, how it changes from morning to afternoon, how it reflects off water, how it filters through leaves. A subject could look completely different depending on the time of day. Monet famously painted the same haystacks dozens of times, at dawn, midday, and dusk, to show how radically light transforms what we see. The goal was never to paint a timeless truth, but a specific, unrepeatable moment.
  • *
    Loose, Visible Brushstrokes Earlier painters blended their paint so smoothly you couldn't see a single brushstroke. Impressionists did the opposite, they left their brushstrokes visible, short, and quick. Up close, the paint looks messy and abstract. Step back, and it resolves into a shimmering, lifelike scene. This technique was shocking and controversial at the time. It's now considered a breakthrough.
  • *
    Everyday Life as Subject Matter Impressionists didn't paint ancient myths or religious miracles. They painted real life, friends at a café, ballet dancers warming up backstage, families picnicking by a river, women reading in gardens. This was radical. It said: the everyday is worth painting. Ordinary moments have beauty and deserve to be remembered. That's a deeply democratic and inclusive idea.
Notable Artists

The Impressionists

A group of friends and rebels who changed art history together.

Claude Monet (1840–1926)

The light-chaser

Monet is the most recognizable name in Impressionism. He was endlessly fascinated by how light transformed nature. His famous Water Lilies series, painted in his own garden in Giverny, shows a pond at different times of day and seasons. Remarkably, he continued painting even as he went nearly blind in his later years.

Edgar Degas (1834–1917)

The movement-capturer

Degas was fascinated with movement and the human body. He is best known for his paintings and sculptures of ballet dancers, not on stage in the spotlight, but rehearsing, stretching, and resting backstage. He captured private, in-between moments that felt genuine and unposed, almost like a photograph.

Pierre-Auguste Renoir (1841–1919)

The celebrator of joy

If Monet painted light and Degas painted movement, Renoir painted joy. His canvases are full of warm color, laughter, dances, and social gatherings. He believed art should be pleasurable and uplifting. His painting Luncheon of the Boating Party is a perfect example, friends eating, drinking, and laughing in dappled sunlight.

Impressionist Masterpiece

"Weeping Willow"

Claude Monet  ·  1919  ·  Oil on Canvas

Monet painted this willow tree during one of the most painful periods in French history, World War I. The war devastated France, and Monet, who had a beloved son serving in the military, felt the grief deeply.

The weeping willow, already a traditional symbol of mourning and grief, was Monet's way of processing sorrow. The drooping, sweeping branches almost look like a figure bent in sadness. The brushstrokes are turbulent and expressive, more emotionally raw than his earlier, sunlit garden paintings.

Even without any human figures, this painting feels like grief. That's the power of Impressionism, communicating emotion through color, texture, and light, not through literal representation.

Takeaway

Why Impressionism Still Matters

Art as Feeling, Not Just Fact

Impressionism changed what art was for. Before it, painting tried to record the world accurately. After Impressionism, painting could record feelings, how a moment felt, not just how it looked. This idea rippled through all of modern art. Abstract art, Expressionism, and even modern photography all owe a debt to the Impressionists who first said: your inner experience of the world is just as real and valid as the world itself.
